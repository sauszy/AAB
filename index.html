Full Discography ↗
1978
Kay Ganda ng Ating Musika
+

Perhaps the most important song in Cayabyab's career — and arguably in OPM history. Composed in 1978, Kay Ganda ng Ating Musika won the Grand Prix at the prestigious Seoul International Song Festival, marking the first time a Filipino composition claimed that honor on the world stage. The song was both a celebration of Filipino music and a declaration: that OPM deserved to stand alongside the best music anywhere in the world. Its win single-handedly elevated the credibility of the entire Original Pilipino Music movement at a time when local artists were still fighting for respect against the dominance of Western pop.

1977
Tuwing Umuulan at Kapiling Ka
+

One of Cayabyab's earliest major compositions, written when he was still a young musician finding his voice. Tuwing Umuulan at Kapiling Ka captures the deeply Filipino emotion of longing — of rain, romance, and the ache of being away from someone you love. The song became an instant classic, demonstrating that Cayabyab had a rare gift for writing melodies that felt both personal and universal. It set the tone for a career defined by emotional honesty and melodic beauty.

▶ YouTube ● Spotify
1978
Kumukutikutitap
+

Kumukutikutitap — a word that itself sparkles and dances when spoken — became one of the most beloved children's songs in Philippine history. Written the same year as Kay Ganda ng Ating Musika, it showed the full range of Cayabyab's talent: he could write a sophisticated award-winning composition for international stages and, in the same breath, craft a simple, joyful melody that generations of Filipino children would grow up singing. It remains a staple of Filipino childhood and a testament to the universality of his musical language.

▶ YouTube ● Spotify
1980s
Paraiso
+

Paraiso is a sweeping, deeply romantic ballad that became one of Cayabyab's signature works of the 1980s. The song reflects his maturing compositional voice — lush, orchestral in feel, and emotionally expansive. It spoke to a generation of Filipinos navigating love, loss, and the longing for something better, something beautiful. The word itself — paradise — carries both hope and melancholy in the Filipino context, and Cayabyab captured that duality with characteristic brilliance.

▶ YouTube ● Spotify
1991
Da Coconut Nut — Smokey Mountain
+

As producer and creative force behind the Smokey Mountain project, Cayabyab helped transform a group of children scavengers from Manila's garbage dumps into one of the most socially significant acts in Philippine music history. Da Coconut Nut became their breakout international hit — a playful, catchy folk-pop song that belied the deeply serious social advocacy at the heart of the project. Smokey Mountain was Cayabyab's proof that music could be a vehicle for social change, giving voice to the voiceless while reaching millions worldwide.

▶ YouTube ● Spotify
1980s
Nais Ko
+

Nais Ko — meaning "What I Want" or "My Wish" — is one of Cayabyab's most introspective compositions. It speaks to the quiet, personal desires that define the human experience: to love, to be loved, to find peace. In an era dominated by loud pop anthems, Nais Ko stood out for its restraint and sincerity. It became a song that Filipinos returned to at life's most meaningful moments — weddings, farewells, reunions — a testament to the timeless emotional truth Cayabyab embedded in its simple, elegant melody.

▶ YouTube ● Spotify
1980
Agila — The Musical
+

Agila (Eagle) marked Cayabyab's bold entry into Philippine musical theater in 1980. The eagle — the Philippine national bird — served as a powerful symbol of national pride and freedom, and Cayabyab's score gave the story its emotional wings. This production signaled that his ambitions went far beyond pop songwriting: he was a complete musical storyteller capable of sustaining dramatic narratives across an entire theatrical work. Agila laid the groundwork for the string of landmark musicals that would cement his reputation as the father of modern Philippine musical theater.

▶ YouTube ● Spotify
1996
Maynila: Isang Musical
+

Maynila: Isang Musical is widely regarded as one of the finest achievements in Philippine musical theater. Premiered in 1996, it tells the story of Manila itself — its contradictions, its beauty, its poverty, its resilience — through Cayabyab's richly layered score. The musical was a love letter and a lament rolled into one, capturing the soul of a city that millions call home. It demonstrated Cayabyab's ability to fuse social commentary with deeply moving music, and it remains a defining work of Filipino cultural expression.

Biography
A Life in Music

Ryan Cayabyab — full name Raymundo Cipriano Pujante Cayabyab — is a living legend of Filipino music: composer, conductor, arranger, and the most enduring architect of Original Pilipino Music (OPM). Honored as National Artist for Music in 2018, his works span beloved hits, socially conscious projects, and landmark musicals that have shaped Philippine culture for five decades.

01
Birth & Family Background
+

Raymundo Cipriano Pujante Cayabyab was born on May 4, 1954, in the heart of Manila, Philippines. He was the youngest in a family that, while not musicians by profession, held a deep and quiet reverence for the arts. Growing up in the city during a period of intense cultural transformation, young Raymundo absorbed the sounds of a Philippines caught between tradition and modernity — folk kundiman drifting from neighbors' radios, brass bands in town plazas, and the influence of American pop filtering through Manila's airwaves after the war.


His parents recognized early that their youngest child had an unusually keen sensitivity to music — he could pick out melodies by ear before he could read sheet music, and he found himself drawn instinctively toward the piano whenever one was nearby. This nurturing home environment, where curiosity was encouraged and artistic expression was never discouraged, became the quiet but powerful foundation upon which one of the Philippines' greatest musical careers would be built.


The nickname "Ryan" — simple, friendly, distinctly different from the formal string of his full name — was the name the world would come to know him by. It suited him perfectly: warm and approachable on the surface, yet carrying something far more complex and remarkable within.

02
Early Exposure to Music & Influence
+

From his earliest years, Ryan Cayabyab was steeped in an extraordinarily diverse musical landscape. Manila in the 1950s and 60s was a city alive with sound — the kundiman and harana of his grandparents' generation mingled with the jazz and swing that American culture had brought to Filipino shores, while the burgeoning folk revival gave voice to a new generation searching for its own identity.


Cayabyab was particularly drawn to the piano, and by his early teens he was already displaying a gift that went beyond mere technical ability — he could hear music architecturally, understanding how notes related to one another, how tension built and resolved, and how emotion could be shaped through rhythm and harmony. He soaked up Western classical music with the same enthusiasm he brought to Filipino folk songs, never seeing any contradiction between the two.


Key early influences included the sacred choral music he heard at church, the popular OPM artists beginning to emerge in the 1960s, and the classical composers he encountered through his formal studies. Beethoven and Brahms sat comfortably alongside Levi Celerio and Francisco Santiago in his musical imagination. This cross-pollination — between the Western and the Filipino, the classical and the popular, the sacred and the secular — would become the defining characteristic of his compositional voice.


It was also during these formative years that Cayabyab developed his lifelong belief that Filipino music deserved to be taken seriously — not as a lesser cousin of Western pop, but as a distinct and worthy art form in its own right. This conviction would fuel everything he did for the next five decades.

03
His Humble Beginnings in the Music Industry
+

Ryan Cayabyab's entry into the professional music world was not marked by overnight fame but by years of quiet, determined work behind the scenes. In the early 1970s, while still a student, he began taking on work as an arranger and session musician — the unglamorous but essential labor that forms the backbone of any music industry. He wrote charts for other artists, arranged songs for recordings, and absorbed the practical craft of music production from the ground up.


His first major breakthrough came in 1977 with Tuwing Umuulan at Kapiling Ka, a song that captured something deeply true about Filipino longing and romance. Its success was not accidental — it was the product of a young composer who had spent years learning exactly how to marry a melody to an emotion. The song became a classic almost immediately, and suddenly the name Ryan Cayabyab meant something.


A year later in 1978, he composed Kay Ganda ng Ating Musika, which would go on to win the Grand Prix at the prestigious Seoul International Song Festival — the first time a Filipino song had claimed that honor. The win was a watershed moment not just for Cayabyab personally, but for the entire Original Pilipino Music movement. It was proof that Filipino music could stand on the world stage and win.


Also in 1978, Kumukutikutitap became one of the most beloved children's songs in Philippine history — a deceptively simple melody that embedded itself permanently in the national consciousness. These three songs in the space of two years established Cayabyab as a once-in-a-generation talent, and set the trajectory for everything that followed.

04
Formal Education & Training in Music
+

Cayabyab pursued his formal music education at the University of the Philippines College of Music, one of the most rigorous and respected music institutions in Southeast Asia. There, he immersed himself in Western classical composition theory, counterpoint, orchestration, and music history — building a technical foundation that would distinguish his work from that of his contemporaries for decades to come.


Under the guidance of distinguished Filipino composers and musicologists, he learned not just how to write music, but how to think about music — historically, culturally, and structurally. The UP College of Music environment also deepened his appreciation for Filipino musical heritage, exposing him to the scholarly study of indigenous and traditional forms that he would later incorporate into his own compositions and arrangements.


His formal training gave him something rare among popular composers: the ability to work fluently across every musical genre and format. He could write a three-minute pop song with the same authority he brought to a full-length choral work or a musical theater score. This versatility — rooted in the comprehensive education he received at UP — became his greatest professional asset.


Beyond technical skills, his years at UP also connected him to a community of artists, intellectuals, and cultural advocates who shared his belief in the importance of Filipino artistic identity. Many of his lifelong collaborators and friends came from this period. The university was not merely a place of study for Cayabyab — it was where his artistic philosophy was formed, and where he first began to see himself not just as a musician, but as a steward of Filipino musical culture.

Career & Legacy
The Maestro's
Enduring Mark
As a National Artist for Music
A Living Cultural Treasure

On August 6, 2018, President Rodrigo Duterte conferred upon Ryan Cayabyab the Order of National Artists for Music — the Philippines' highest honor for artists. This recognition formalized what the Filipino public had known for decades: that Cayabyab is not merely a musician, but a cultural institution. His contributions span original compositions, choral direction, musical theater, music production, and education — making him one of the most complete and consequential figures in Philippine arts history. Through his work with the Ryan Cayabyab Singers, his landmark musicals such as Katy! The Musical, Maynila: Isang Musical, and San Pedro Calungsod Musical, and his transformative role as a mentor and educator, he has shaped multiple generations of Filipino musicians.

Legacy and Contribution
Building OPM
"He did not just write songs. He gave Filipino music its dignity."

From advocating for Original Pilipino Music at a time when Western pop dominated the airwaves, to producing the socially transformative Smokey Mountain project, to mentoring a generation of artists through the Ryan Cayabyab Singers — his legacy is inseparable from the story of Filipino cultural identity. He proved that local music could be both commercially resonant and artistically profound.
